About me

My name is Danilo Ferreira, and I am currently a postdoctoral researcher at the Laboratório Nacional de Astrofísica (LNA), Brazil. I work in observational stellar astrophysics, with a focus on massive stars, particularly Be stars and hierarchical multiple systems, using photometric, spectroscopic, and interferometric data in the optical and near-infrared. I hold a PhD in Astronomy from the Observatório Nacional (2025), including a research internship at the Observatoire de Paris (2024), as well as an MSc in Astrophysics and a BSc in Physics. Alongside research, I am actively involved in science outreach and education. Outside academia, I enjoy listening to music (MPB and Brazilian rock), going to the beach, and playing electronic games.

Research Interests

During my Master’s and PhD research, I worked on OB and OBe stars using large photometric datasets, mainly from the TESS mission, combined with spectroscopic observations to identify stellar companions and constrain orbital parameters. This research provided strong experience in collaborative, data-driven projects and in interpreting results within the framework of stellar evolution and angular momentum transport. I made extensive use of the Wilson–Devinney code for binary star modeling, developing a Python interface to the original Fortran implementation coupled with the emcee package for robust parameter estimation. In addition, I developed Python tools to measure radial velocities, derive stellar atmospheric parameters, process and analyze TESS photometric data, and construct Hertzsprung–Russell diagrams.

A significant part of my work focused on the massive eclipsing binary W36, for which I performed a detailed photometric and spectroscopic analysis to determine its orbital and physical properties. By modeling the system with the Wilson–Devinney code combined with MCMC techniques, we derived precise stellar masses, effective temperatures, and an age estimate for the massive cluster Westerlund 1 of approximately 5–12 million years, providing strong constraints on its recent star formation history.

I also investigated the hierarchical triple system V1371 Tau, composed of an inner eclipsing binary and an outer classical Be star. Using TESS photometry, multi-epoch spectroscopy, and interferometric data, I confirmed its hierarchical configuration and characterized both the inner and outer orbits. The analysis showed that the Be star is a fast rotator with a projected rotational velocity of about 250 km/s and hosts a Keplerian circumstellar disk with weak H-alpha emission. The system presents evidence of dynamical interactions, possibly including Kozai–Lidov cycles, affecting the inclination of the inner binary, offering valuable observational insight into the dynamics and evolution of massive multiple systems with rapidly rotating components.

Outreach

Since I began studying science, especially astronomy, I have had a deep motivation to share my enthusiasm for the cosmos. My first experiences in science outreach involved presenting physics experiments, operating telescopes and planetariums, bringing knowledge to the most isolated cities of Alagoas, Brazil, to children, young people, adults, and the elderly.
